Brief History of Linux

Linux is an open-source operating system kernel first released by Linus Torvalds on September 17, 1991. It was developed as a free and open alternative to proprietary UNIX systems. Torvalds, a student at the University of Helsinki, started the project as a hobby but soon attracted contributions from developers worldwide.

Initially, Linux was just a kernel, requiring external components like the GNU utilities to form a complete operating system. The adoption of the GNU General Public License (GPL) in 1992 ensured that Linux remained free and open-source, leading to the rapid growth of its community and ecosystem.

By the mid-1990s, several distributions (distros) emerged, making Linux more accessible. Debian, Slackware, and Red Hat became some of the most influential early distributions. Linux gained traction in academic and enterprise environments due to its stability, security, and cost-effectiveness.

During the 2000s, Linux saw significant adoption in server environments, with major corporations like IBM and Oracle providing support. The rise of cloud computing further propelled Linux into mainstream use, as it became the foundation for platforms like Amazon Web Services (AWS) and Google Cloud.

In the 2010s, Linux became ubiquitous in mobile computing through Android, which is based on the Linux kernel. Today, Linux continues to be a cornerstone of modern computing, known for its flexibility, efficiency, and strong community support.
